Limiting Factors

There are several possible limiting factors to this conclusion.

  • sample size: the sample size being just 236 students out of the 30,000+ students at UCLA may be too small to produce any valid generalized results.
  • confounding variables: there are several other confounding variables that were not taken into account in determining each students' GPA, such as their daily study time, whether or not they like their degree, if they have a part time job, their stress level, and their financial status. These could all impact a students' GPA to be either higher or lower.
  • self-report bias: if this survey data was self-reported, there could be bias in some students' GPA. This bias could cause an overestimation of GPAs.

The higher levels of stress amongst the financially well-off students may stem from other factors, such as pressure from parents, higher expectations to maintain, competitive environments/mindsets, or the additional responsibility that comes with managing wealth.

To conclude, the data suggests that higher financial status can increase stress, challenging the notion that wealth inherently leads to a better or less stressful personal life. The evidence does not strongly suggest a correlation between wealth and academic performance.

Limiting Factors:

  • self-reported: Since this data was self-reported by students, there may be bias in how students interpret their own financial status and stress level. This can result in either an over or under estimate of correlation between financial status and personal well-being.
  • confounding variables: There are several confounding variables that could have a potential effect on students' academic, professional, and personal well-being that were not taken into consideration, such as their study habits, family situation, quality of life, etc.
